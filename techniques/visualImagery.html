Visual Imagery

Target Age Group: 5-18 year olds

Objective: Students learn the process of visual imagery as a technique to help themselves calm down.

Why This Works:


  • Grounding:
    • Helps students feel safe in their own space and let go of pent-up emotions
    • Encourages deep breathing techniques

  • Symbolic Connection:
    • Provides a safe place they can imagine alone or open up to others they’ve lost or been separated from

  • Mindfulness-Based:
    • Encourages awareness, stress release, and emotional regulation

  • Universal & Simple:
    • Requires no materials, is highly personalizable, and easy to teach

Instructions:


  • Get Comfortable:
    • Students sit comfortably for this activity

  • Use a Calm Voice:
    • Teacher should speak in a calm, low, slow voice
    • Allow sufficient time between each suggestion so students can “ease” into the vision

  • Create a Scenario:
    • Build a visual story appropriate for students’ age, experience, and interests
    • Example:
      • “Close your eyes and see in your mind’s eye a beautiful beach.”
      • “Imagine the sun shining, a warm breeze, palm trees overhead, and seagulls circling.”
      • “Walk barefoot in the sand, feel your feet sink with each step, and let the water roll over your feet.”
      • “Swim in the warm, refreshing ocean, then lay down on your towel and relax.”
      • “Notice how it looks, sounds, smells—breathe in deeply and stay as long as you like.”
      • “When you’re ready, throw your burdens into the ocean—anything that’s bothering you.”
      • “Picture it as a rock, chain, or heavy bag, then watch it sink and be taken away by the waves.”
      • “Be thankful for the release, then walk peacefully back and rest.”

  • Practice:
    • Do this activity for 10–20 minutes, depending on grade level

  • Debrief:
    • Give students time to share their experiences in pairs, small groups, or whole class
    • Guiding questions include:
      • Where did your visualization take you?
      • Do you feel calmer or more relaxed?
      • What did you see, hear, feel, or smell?
      • What was it like throwing your burdens into the water?
      • How does it feel being back in the classroom?
      • Is this something you might use in the future to relax?

  • Extension:
    • Practice visualizations several times until students achieve competence
    • Encourage students to use this at home or when stressed
    • Reinforce by having them write or journal their post-visualization feelings
    • Students may create their own visualizations and take turns leading the class

  • Follow-Up Impact:
    • Encourages reflection and provides a tool students can carry with them
